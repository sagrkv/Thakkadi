## What is a Limitation Period?

A limitation period is the maximum time allowed by law to file a suit, appeal, or application in court. Once this period expires, the court will refuse to hear your case — regardless of its merits. In India, limitation periods are governed primarily by **The Limitation Act, 1963**.

The Act contains a Schedule divided into three parts:
- **Division I** — Suits (e.g., recovery of money, possession of property)
- **Division II** — Appeals (e.g., first appeal, second appeal, criminal appeals)
- **Division III** — Applications (e.g., review, execution, revision)

## Why Limitation Periods Matter

Consider this scenario: A Sessions Court convicts your client. You have **30 days** to file an appeal to the High Court under Article 116 of the Limitation Act. If you file on Day 31, the appeal is time-barred.

There are provisions for condonation of delay under Section 5 of the Limitation Act, but these are discretionary — the court may or may not grant it. The safest approach is always to file within time.

## Key Limitation Periods for Post-Judgment Actions

| Action | Period | Law |
|--------|--------|-----|
| First appeal to High Court | 90 days | Article 116(a), Limitation Act |
| First appeal to District Court | 30 days | Article 116(b), Limitation Act |
| Criminal appeal to Sessions Court | 30 days | Article 115(c), Limitation Act |
| Review petition | 30 days | Article 124, Limitation Act |
| Revision petition | 90 days (by analogy) | Article 131, Limitation Act |
| Execution of decree | 12 years | Article 133, Limitation Act |
| SLP to Supreme Court | 90 days (civil) / 60 days (criminal) | SC Rules 2013 |

## The Copy Exclusion Rule

One important rule that many lawyers overlook: **the time spent obtaining a certified copy of the judgment is excluded** from the limitation period. This is provided under Section 12 of the Limitation Act.

So if you apply for a certified copy on Day 1 and receive it on Day 15, those 15 days are excluded. Your limitation clock starts from Day 16 (effectively).

## Important Caveat

Limitation periods can vary based on specific circumstances, court rules, and judicial interpretation. Special statutes (like the Commercial Courts Act or Family Courts Act) sometimes prescribe shorter periods that override the Limitation Act. Always verify with the applicable statute and consult a qualified legal professional.
